# Getting Started with the OneMuseum

**NOTE: This is the Pre-Release version which includes only part of the total offerings of OneMuseum and which has been made available to our testers to assess and provide guidance.**

This page provides the history and of OneMuseum and the motivation for it development, with a high-level description of the functions and design principles.

## The ETHER Initiative

Good day to you and thank-you for visiting the OneMuseum.

I am Dr Roger Layton, and I have conceptualised and built the OneMuseum together with my team, for the purpose of making the wealth of information and knowledge in museums available to the world at large.

Fifteen years ago, in 2005, I created the specialised heritage consultancy **ETHER** -a mnemonic for **Et**ernal **Her**itage - with the mission of transforming museums and similar heritage institutions from physical to digital. During this time we have expanded the range of our services into training and application systems, with two core objectives: (1) documenting and preserving collections, and (2) making collections available using digital technologies.

ETHER is a business unit within Roger Layton Associates (RLA), a Johannesburg-based organisation, specializing in the heritage and educational sectors.

The OneMuseum is offered a free service: free for museums to host the information; free for users to access the information. This free hosting and access is provided on a "fair use" basis, to enable the resources to be spread as widely as possible.

The costs of hosting are covered by RLA/ETHER. and as and when these costs increase we will explore funding opportunities outside of the company.

As the OneMuseum grows in its hosted content, and its access usage, we will be adding certain commercial services over and above the free services to assist with the operational costs.

## About you

You may be a staffer working in a museum that is looking for opportunities to publish its content digitally, to make this content accessible in these days when access to museums physically is all but impossible.

You may be a school-level learner or researcher looking to find information from a museum that is not available without having to travel to the museum to obtain the information you are looking for.

One challenge has been that that the museum community has been very slow transforming to digital, given that this can be a significant cost and effort while funding for heritage is increasingly difficult to obtain.

Another challenge, and a more pressing one  in my opinion, is that the wealth of the trusted and curated information within museums is almost completely unavailable to the world at large - a world of today which depends online access to information. The world of users are having to use potentially untrusted sources of information, when they should be sourcing their information from the most trusted of all institutions, including the museums, archives, and libraries of our world.

## What is the OneMuseum

The OneMuseum is an information portal which combines the information from museums into a single data structure, making this available all users.

This is a digital museum-of-museums which is not merely a library of images of items and documents, but which rather contains a range of types of data and information, including raw data and interpreted information, to serve the needs of education and research and for general public interest. So you will be able to see the raw content of the individual items in the collections, as well as the curated stories which bind these items to the history they represent.

## About the BETA version of OneMuseum

The capabilities of OneMuseum are being introduced gradually, as we negotiate with museums to gain access to their content, and as the needs of the users become more apparent over time.

Our first offering is this BETA version which we are using for live testing of the OneMuseum.

The focus of this initial release is on school-level education. This will provide digital access to the contents and the experiences which are provide during schools visits to museums. We expect that such field trips may not be reinstated for a long time due to the social distancing requirements of the coronavirus. However, even without the physical visits, the needs remainsfor learners to gain exposure to the experiences at the museums, and museums can still support this need through the OneMuseum Lessons and curriculum linkages.

Our vision is that all museums will agree to host their educational materials on OneMuseum, to provide ease of access to the educators to find suitable trusted references information for their lessons.

Our approach comprises the following activities:

* adopting museums into the OneMuseum family, providing basic information about the museum
* engaging with education officers in the museums to prepare their lesson support content for uploading to OneMuseum
* linkage of this content to the curriculum, with us having already loaded the entire CAPS curriculum into OneMuseum at the level of the topics and sections
* linkage of the content to other categories, as explained below
* promoting the OneMuseum within the education community as a trusted source of educational materials and reference information
* providing citations back to the content to enable learners and researchers to provide linkage back to the sources of their referenced materials
* providing feedback to the museums on the extent of digital access to the content they have provided, and on the types of queries which users are looking for

The OneMuseum is described in the [User Guide]('/s/userguide') and the description of the adoption of museums and their content is descriped in the [Guide for Contributing Museums]('s/museumguid').

## Categorization

One of the most challenging aspect of the development of OneMuseum has been to create a universal method of categorisation of the all content hosted within OneMuseum. This will supplement existing methods of categorization in use by the individuals museums. The purpose of this categorization approach is to ensure that there are common descriptions of the content.

We have implemented six classes of categories, with the capability to add more as the needs arise.

For example, one of these classes is **Curriculum Elements**, which covers the major topics in each subject curriculum in each grade, so that a lesson provided by a museum can be linked directly to the right place in the curriculum. This allows a teacher to select the topic they are engaged with and find all content which mas been categorized as supporting this topic. 

The set of these classes of categories is open-ended and currently constitutes the following, each of which is managed as a controlled vocabulary by ourselves, and each of which is in a constant state of growth and renewal. THe six classes currenntly implemented are:

* **Named Events**: covering named time periods both specific and approximate, such as geological time (Jurassic), archaeological time (Early Stone Age), historical time (Reformation), or military time (World War II), and specific events (Sharpeville Massacre).
* **Named Places**: which covers place names such as countries, towns, cities, both modern and ancient, down the level of streets if required, as well as the names of natural places (rivers, mountains, oceans, planets).
* **Agents**: being persons or organisations with whom content is associated, this could be potentially large and is evolving as museums add content linked to a person. Such persons will include both well-known and not-so-well-known people, and organisations will including companies, political organisations, tribes).
* **Subjects**: a general-purpose list of subjects which do not fit into the other classes, such as disciplines (Statistics, Psychology, Physics), a list of human activities (running, picnicking, family, health), and almost any other type of subject-related knowledge.
* **Item Types**: for situations in which a specific type of item is being addressed by the content, such as clothing, firearms, etc. This is a high-level list, and not a fine-grained listing such as Chenhall (see below).
* **Curriculum Elements**: as discussed above - for every element of the CAPS curriculum, and also any other curriculum from any country of the world as this is expanded to include curricula from throughout the world.

We are in the process of including other classes to support categorisation, including:

* **Taxonomies**: for natural science specimens
* **Named Objects**: for particular buildings, sites, or object which are identified by a common name
* **DEWEY**: for categorization of publications

## Content provided by museums

We have designed the OneMuseum to move beyond the traditional forms of information provided my museums, to include additional forms which are suited for digital access.

Every museum will commence with a little information and will grow to include more over time and as suits the needs of the museum. Ideally all content should be provided eventually, with dynamic updating from the museum's collection management systems. Our initial focus is on the Lessons, to support teaching and learning.

The types of information currently provided are the following:

* **Museum Information**: about the museum itself, its history, highlights, main categories of collections, etc.
* **Collections**: the various collections of the museum, described in terms of history, contents, highlights, and by the categories which they are linked to.
* **Items**: the full catalogue of items in each collection, with fields representing the type of the items.
* **Exhibitions**: for both physical exhiition and virtual exhibitions, placing these online with a guided tour to enable virtual access to curated exhibitions.
* **Lessons**: specific content for teaching and learning support, including specific lessons, and reference materials to support curriculum topics.
* **Biographies**: many museums will feature specific people of interest, or organisations, and biographical information with reference back to other holdings, such as archival documents and letters, provide a depth of information far beyond what is available from WikiPedia.
* **Expert**: the list of experts associated with the museum.
* **Stories**: small, bite-sized stories which use the holdings of the museum to tell stories which can be used to enliven the content and provide historical context.  
* **Publications**: access to the formal pulication of the museum, including reearch reports and articles published in journals by the staff.
* **Museum Events**: a calendar of events to be conducted by the museum, including future online lectures and talks, including talks linked to the curriculum.

## Reference Information

Our goal is to provide additional information to support the museums, such as international standards.

At present we have a single reference data set:

### Chenhall Nomenclature

This is the definitive vocabulary for the naming of cultural objects in museums, based on the original usage of objects and structured into a six-level hierarchy. We make this available a search engine and navigation through the hierarchy. This reference data sets assists museums in finding the standard name items to be added.

## What you can do as a user

We provide two user functionsin the Pre-Release version:

* marking an item found as a favourite
* quick access to the list of favourites

### Searching

Any of the content can be searched, for example Lessons provided by the educational functions of museums can be searched for items which meet a particular requirement.

Alternatively, a search can be conducted on a Curriculum Element, and using this to show the Lessons and other content which has been linked to this element.

### Marking as favourites

A click on the star icon will select or delect an item as a a favourite.

### Showing your favourites

All of the items you have marked as a favourite will appear in a list which you can use to quickly return to the items of interest to you.
